COVID-19 Quarantine, Part 1

Sun, Jul 12, 2020 6 min read Monthly Updates

Since I’ve hunkered down in my apartment, quite a bit has happened in the world. The good news is that the weather in San Diego is gorgeous! However, 2020 seems to be throwing every possible scenario at us. These days, it feels like I’m at the center of a whirlwind of news headlines, attempts at productivity, and mental instability. And to be honest, I haven’t been posting to this blog because I haven’t quite learned how to consolidate my thoughts.

Let’s hope this attempt is somewhat intelligible—at least for myself in the future. I’m splitting this update into two posts because I felt that it would go a tad bit long.

Passing my 2nd Year Qualifying Examination

I’ll start with the easy news first—I passed my 2nd Year Qualifying Exam!

Within the Bioinformatics & Systems Biology program at UC San Diego, the 2nd Year Qualifying Exam (or what students call “quals” for short), is a milestone that all students should pass to continue their graduate study. According to my program’s website:

The second-year qualifying examination (BQE) is designed to examine the student’s ability to think critically, analytically, and independently, and to apply the skills acquired in classes to a real research project. The subject of the exam is the student’s current research project, but the focus is the student’s critical analytical ability and command of relevant methods and subjects. In this vein, committee members may ask general questions that, while originally prompted by the student’s proposal, are intended to test the student’s depth of knowledge in significant areas of bioinformatics or systems biology.

— https://bioinformatics.ucsd.edu/node/37

For this exam, students must submit a 10-page proposal detailing a project they plan to pursue in their thesis lab and then present this proposal to a committee of three randomly selected faculty affiliated with our program. Because bioinformatics is very multidisciplinary—calling from concepts in biology, computer science, and statistics—students are usually nervous about who is assigned to evaluate them. A professor’s background can dictate the types of questions they would ask about a project. In this year’s round of quals, there was also an added stressor caused by the COVID-19 pandemic: students were nervous about presenting their research over a Zoom call instead of in-person!

Professors Pavel Pevzner, Christopher Benner, and Albert Hsiao were assigned to my qual committee. When I got my assignment, I was extremely nervous because my project touched on subject areas that each of these faculty are experts of! Fortunately, things ended up running smoothly, and in retrospect, their expertise in areas of my project helped me think about my project in ways I hadn’t focused on before.

For my qual, I presented my recent work in the Farley Lab that utilizes single-cell technologies to study Ciona intestinalis development. I also discussed future steps for my project and the approaches I would be taking. (I can’t talk about my project quite yet here, but I hope to soon, don’t worry!) Prof. Pevzner asked questions testing my algorithmic knowledge and how I would be applying that knowledge to my project. On the other hand, Prof. Benner and Prof. Hsiao were concerned about the implications of my research and biological conclusions I would be able to make from future analyses.

However, all of the faculty on my committee were concerned about my ability to grow as an independent scientist. To them, it felt like my experimental collaborators in the Farley Lab dictated the project and goals I have for my research moving forward. And to be honest, a part of me is worried about being used as a means to analyze data in a way that follows somebody else’s vision. Going into my qual, I was already feeling nervous about having adequate computational guidance in my biology-focused thesis lab and having agency in collaborative projects. The feedback from my qual solidified this concern in my head and moving forward, and I’ll always be mindful of what it means to be a computational trainee in an experimental lab.

Reflecting on COVID-19 Quarantine

At this point, I’ve been in quarantine since March 13th, 2020—121 days. As the weeks have gone by, I’ve felt my schedule become more delayed: in March, I started my day at around 6:00 AM, but now, I start my day at around 9:30 AM to 10:00 AM at times! Since every day feels like a weekend, it’s hard to feel productive or motivated to do more than an hour of intense, focused work.

I also felt significant imposter syndrome when we first started quarantine. For some reason, it felt like my peers were uber-productive despite everything going on. And some were! At first, at the very least. Early on, we all felt like now was the time to focus on research and classes. We initially were getting things done. But after talking with friends and other graduate students, I’ve come to realize that getting work done during this time of uncertainty should be considered an extra benefit. During these times, it feels like we’re putting in more hours than usual, and it feels harder to set boundaries with regards to “working hours” when our own living spaces serve as an office.

With higher expectations to be more productive and work longer hours, I’m at the constant edge of suffering from burn-out. I recently took four days off from work, and while it may have helped somewhat, I still felt pressure to check email and notifications and complete things that are “productive.”

However, I do think that there are some benefits to working from home, at least for my working style. When sitting at my desk on-campus, I’m surrounded by the noise of bustling equipment and bench scientists conducting experiments. Sometimes, people swing by my desk to quickly as questions or check in on my progress for specific projects. When you’re in the flow of programming, this open-office format and its idiosyncrasies can be distracting! Working from home is the equivalent to finally having my own dry-lab office space, free from distractions.

I hope that when we move back to in-person work, I’ll be allowed to work from home or away from the laboratory once in a while! It’s all a balancing act: I think that some hybrid between in-person and remote would help me be more efficient in my work while setting concrete boundaries between work and personal life.

See you in my second post in this large update on what’s been going on in my graduate school adventures!

Michelle Franc Ragsac
Bioinformatics PhD Student

My research interests include early embryonic development, computational genomics, and single-cell technologies.
